# Quantify Mobile — Design Workshop

# Shipments: Delivery & Return



## What this is

A single 3-hour workshop with the Quantify team. One session. Everyone contributes. We walk out with clear design direction for the mobile shipments feature.



## Who it's for

Developers, product managers, and support staff at Quantify. People who know the product deeply but haven't been through a design process before.



## Why one session?

We don't have access to yard workers. We can't run field research right now. But we have something almost as valuable: a team full of people who talk to customers every day, who fix their problems, who know the edge cases. The goal is to get that knowledge out of individual heads and into a shared picture that the design can be built on.

---

# Before the Session



## What the facilitator prepares

A printed one-pager (or shared screen) with:

- The scope: mobile app for confirming deliveries and returns. Counting items on a tablet in the yard. That's it.
- What's NOT in scope: creating shipments, billing, invoicing, reports, scanning. Desktop only.
- The 4 open design questions we need to answer by the end (listed below).

A shared board — FigJam, Miro, or a physical whiteboard. Pre-drawn with the activities laid out so people can see the whole session at a glance. Nothing hidden. No surprises.

Sticky notes and markers for everyone (physical or digital). If digital, send the FigJam link the day before so people can test it works.



## What participants need to know beforehand

Send this message 2 days before:

> We're running a 3-hour workshop to figure out how the Quantify mobile app should work for shipments. You don't need to prepare anything. You don't need design experience. You need to bring what you already know — the weird edge cases, the support tickets that keep coming back, the thing about the workflow that has always bugged you. That's the input. We'll do the rest together.

That's it. No homework. No pre-reading. If they show up with their existing knowledge, that's enough.

# The People in the Room

| Role | Why they're here |
| :--- | :--- |
| Product manager | Knows what's been promised, what's been prioritised, what the business cares about |
| Developer(s) | Knows what's technically easy and what's a 3-month problem. Says "that's hard" early so we don't fall in love with the wrong idea |
| Support / CS | The closest thing we have to end users. Hears complaints every day. Knows what breaks, what confuses people, what gets asked repeatedly |
| Designer / facilitator | Runs the session, captures everything, turns the output into designs after |

Ideal size: 4–7 people. Fewer than 4 and you don't get enough perspectives. More than 7 and people stop talking.

# Activity 1: Opening + Warm-up

Duration: 15 minutes



## What the facilitator says

"This is a design workshop, but you don't need to be a designer. There are no right answers. We're here because each of you knows something about this product and these users that nobody else in the room knows. My job is to get that knowledge onto the board."



## Three rules for the session

1. No idea is bad. If it's technically impossible, the developer will tell us — but the idea still counts.
2. Sticky notes, not speeches. Write it down, put it up. We discuss after.
3. When we vote, everyone gets equal votes. Title doesn't matter.



## Warm-up question

Go around the room, 1 minute each:

> "What's the last support ticket or customer complaint about shipments that stuck with you?"

This gets people talking about real problems immediately. It also surfaces the knowledge that support and CS carry but rarely get asked about in product conversations.

---

# Activity 2: The User We're Designing For

Duration: 20 minutes

We can't talk to yard workers, but we can build a picture of them from what the team already knows.



## How it works

The facilitator draws a simple card on the board with 5 fields:

**Name:** Give them a name — "Dave" or "Maria" — makes it real.

**Job:** What do they actually do all day?

**Tools today:** Paper pick ticket, radio, phone, desktop Quantify?

**Biggest hassle:** The thing that makes their job harder than it should be.

**Tech comfort:** Are they on smartphones? Do they use apps for other things?

Go around the room. Each person fills in what they know or what they've heard from customers. The support person will carry this one — let them.

If there's disagreement ("some yards are tech-savvy, some aren't"), capture both. Make two cards. That's fine. We're designing for the less tech-savvy one.



## Output

1–2 user cards pinned at the top of the board. They stay visible for the rest of the session. Every decision references them: "Would Dave understand this?"

---

# Activity 3: Map the Workflow

Duration: 30 minutes



## How it works

Draw a horizontal line on the board. Label the endpoints:

**Truck arrives** → ??? → **Office has confirmed numbers**

Ask the room: "What happens in between? One step per sticky note."

Everyone writes sticky notes simultaneously (3 minutes, in silence). Then take turns putting them on the timeline. The facilitator arranges them in order, asks "is anything missing?", and fills gaps.

The final map should be 8–15 steps. It will look something like:

> Truck arrives → Worker checks schedule → Finds the pick ticket → Goes to the truck → Counts items → Marks discrepancies on paper → Calls office if something is wrong → Office updates Quantify → Truck leaves



## Key facilitator move

Ask the support person: "Where in this timeline do things go wrong?" Mark those steps with a red dot.



## Output

A shared workflow map that everyone agrees on. Red dots on the pain points.

# Break

10 minutes.

# Activity 4: Pain Points + Opportunities

Duration: 25 minutes



## How it works

Set up a two-column board:

| What breaks? | What if we could...? |
| :--- | :--- |
| (pain points go here) | (opportunities go here) |

Everyone gets 5 minutes to silently write sticky notes for both columns. One problem or one opportunity per note. Then put them all up.

The facilitator clusters them (takes about 5 minutes). Read each cluster aloud.



## Dot vote

Everyone gets 3 dots. Put them on the clusters you think are most important for the mobile app to solve. You can stack all 3 on one thing if you feel strongly.

Circle the top 3 clusters. These become the design priorities.



## Output

3 prioritised problems or opportunities that the mobile design must address.

---

# Activity 5: Sketch Solutions

Duration: 25 minutes

This is the part where people get nervous. The facilitator's job is to kill that nervousness immediately.



## What the facilitator says

"We're going to sketch. Not draw. Not design. Sketch. Boxes, arrows, and words. If you can draw a rectangle and write a label inside it, you can do this. I'll show you what I mean."

The facilitator draws an example on the board in 30 seconds — a simple screen with a title, two list items, and a button. Boxes with text inside them. Nothing fancy.

"That's it. Boxes. Labels. Arrows. That's what a sketch looks like. You're not being judged on how it looks. You're being judged on whether the idea is interesting."



## How it works

Each person picks one moment from the workflow (the list screen, the counting screen, the discrepancy screen, the confirm screen — wherever they feel they have an opinion) and sketches what they think that screen should look like.

Rules:

- 10 minutes, in silence.
- One screen only — not the whole app.
- Write a title at the top: what screen is this?
- Write one sentence at the bottom: what's the key idea?

After 10 minutes: pin all sketches on the wall or share screens.

**No presenting.** Silent gallery walk. Everyone looks at everyone's sketches for 3 minutes and puts a dot on anything they find interesting.

The facilitator then calls out what got dots and asks: "What did you like about this?" — directed at the voters, not the sketcher. This takes the pressure off the person who drew it.



## Output

5–7 sketched screens, dot-voted, with the team's instincts visible.

---

# Activity 6: Vote + Discuss

Duration: 20 minutes



## How it works

The facilitator synthesises what the team is converging on:

"Looking at the votes, here's what I'm seeing: most of you put the shipment list as a simple today-only view. Two sketches showed the counting screen as a line-by-line checklist rather than a summary. The confirm screen had a signature in 3 out of 5 sketches. Does that feel right?"

Open the floor for 10 minutes of discussion. The facilitator's job here is to capture disagreements specifically — not to resolve them. If two people disagree on the counting screen, write both positions on the board. That's input for the next activity.

---

# Activity 7: Decide the 4 Open Questions

Duration: 25 minutes

This is the business end of the session. These questions must be answered before design can move forward.

The facilitator puts each question on the board, one at a time. For each: 2 minutes of discussion, then the product manager (or whoever the decision owner is) makes the call.



## Question 1: Signature

When a delivery needs customer sign-off, should it be a drawn signature on screen or is a typed name enough?

- What support knows: what do customers currently accept?
- What the developer knows: how hard is drawn signature to implement?

**Decision:** _______________



## Question 2: Unannounced trucks

Can a worker start counting for a shipment that hasn't been created in Quantify yet?

- What support knows: how often does this happen?
- What the PM knows: is this a business risk or a business need?

**Decision:** _______________



## Question 3: Offline

If there's no signal in the yard, should counting still work and sync later?

- What the developer knows: how hard is offline-first?
- What support knows: do customers ever mention connectivity problems?

**Decision:** _______________



## Question 4: Schedule window

How far ahead should scheduled shipments appear: today only, or the next few days?

- What support knows: do yards plan ahead or work day-by-day?

**Decision:** _______________

Write each decision on the board in marker. Take a photo. This is the contract.



## Output

4 written decisions with reasoning.

---

# Activity 8: Wrap-up

Duration: 10 minutes

The facilitator recaps by pointing to each output on the board:

- Here's the user we're designing for.
- Here's the workflow.
- Here are the 3 things the mobile app must solve.
- Here are the sketches the team gravitated toward.
- Here are the 4 decisions.

"I'm going to take all of this and turn it into wireframes. You'll see them within [timeframe]. When you do, your job is to look at them and ask: does this match what we said today? If not, tell me."

Thank everyone. Name one specific contribution from each person that moved the session forward. This sounds small but it matters — it tells people their input was actually used, which makes them show up next time.

# After the Session



## What the designer does (within 48 hours)

1. Photograph or export everything from the board.
2. Write a 1-page summary: user card, workflow map, 3 priorities, 4 decisions, sketch highlights.
3. Share the summary with everyone who was in the room — and anyone who wasn't but needs to know.
4. Start wireframing from the sketches and decisions — not from scratch.



## What everyone else does

Look at the wireframes when they arrive. React to them. "This isn't what we said" is the most valuable feedback at this stage.

# The 4 Outputs

| Output | What it is | Why it matters |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| User card | 1–2 profiles of who we're designing for | Every design decision has a face to check against |
| Workflow map | The step-by-step process, with pain points marked | Shared understanding — no more "I thought it worked differently" |
| 3 design priorities | The most important problems to solve, team-voted | Focus — we build these first, everything else waits |
| 4 design decisions | Signature, offline, unannounced trucks, schedule window — answered | Unblocks wireframing immediately |

# Why This Works Without End Users

It's not ideal. Direct user research is always better. But this team has years of accumulated proxy knowledge:

- **Support** hears complaints every week. They know what's confusing, what breaks, what people ask for.
- **Developers** have been maintaining Quantify for years. They know the data model, the edge cases, the things that "shouldn't happen but do."
- **PMs** know what's been requested, what's been deprioritised, and why.

A workshop puts all of that knowledge in one room for 3 hours instead of leaving it scattered across Slack threads and Jira tickets. That's the value.

The risk: we're designing based on what we think users need, not what we've observed. Acknowledge that. When the prototype is ready, if there's any opportunity to put it in front of even one real yard worker — take it. One 20-minute session with one user is worth more than a month of internal assumptions.

# Facilitator Cheat Sheet

**If people aren't writing sticky notes:**
Write one yourself out loud as a demo. "I'm going to write: 'Workers call the office when counts don't match.' Who has another one?"

**If one person dominates:**
"I want to hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet. [Name], what do you think?"

**If sketching makes people freeze:**
Walk over with your own terrible sketch and say "Mine looks like this — boxes and words. That's all you need."

**If a discussion spirals:**
"I'm going to capture this as two options on the board. We'll vote on it in the next activity."

**If someone says "users would never...":**
"How do you know? Let's write that as an assumption and flag it for testing later."

**If you're running behind:**
Cut discussion time, never activity time. The activities produce the outputs. The discussions are clarification.
